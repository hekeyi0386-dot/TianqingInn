Xie Yinwen

As Xie Yingwen's footsteps touched the bluestone paths of Tianqing Inn, the city dust clinging to his soles finally seemed to find a place to settle. The wind, thick with the moist scent of plants and trees, brushed over his tense shoulders and neck, drawing an unconscious sigh of relief from him—a sense of relaxation he had never known in his role as a product developer buried in a sea of code.

He still remembered the day he was laid off: the HR manager's gentle yet unyielding tone, the project error report spread out on the table, its dense annotations piercing his eyes like needles. In those days, he had glued himself to his desk, staring at the dancing data and ever-updating requirements on his screen. Even when he closed his eyes, all he saw were product iteration flowcharts. Pressure had surged over him like a tide—first insomnia, then night-long palpitations, until that final critical mistake became the last straw. The layoff hadn't been a surprise. He knew deep down that his overtaxed body and numbed nerves had long since been unable to sustain the job he once loved.

On the day he left the office building, he had muted his phone and stuffed it into the deepest corner of his backpack. Once essential tools for survival, these electronic devices now felt like death warrants, reminding him of the days filled with KPIs and lost identity. He just wanted to find a place where he could breathe quietly, hear the sound of his own heartbeat, and piece back together the parts of himself that work had erased.

The gate of Tianqing Inn was slightly ajar, letting out a soft creak as he pushed it open. The roses in the courtyard corner were in full bloom, and a few chicks wandered leisurely along the base of the wall. The innkeeper poked his head out from the house, smiling and asking if he was here to stay, his tone as warm as a family member's. Xie Yingwen nodded, his throat tightening as he found himself momentarily speechless—it had been far too long since he had felt such uncalculating, pure kindness.

He chose a room at the back of the inn, with a view of a grove of trees outside the window. The first thing he did after setting down his backpack was to take out his phone, turn it off, and tuck it into a hidden compartment of his suitcase. When he finished, he leaned against the window, watching sunlight filter through the gaps in the bamboo leaves to dapple the ground, and for the first time in a long while, he felt at peace.

Perhaps here, he could sleep soundly, no longer jolted awake by work messages in the early morning. Perhaps here, he could learn to tend to flowers and plants, feeling the power of growing life. Perhaps here, he could pick up the writing he once loved, putting down the emotions he had neglected for work. He didn't expect to immediately find his life's direction—he only hoped that in this tranquility, he could reconcile with himself and rediscover the sincere, passionate person he had been before pressure had swallowed him whole.
